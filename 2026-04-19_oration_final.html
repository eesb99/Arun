LODGE OF SORROW ORATION

In Memory of Bro. Dr. Arun Bag, Past Master -- Lodge Kilwinning in the East No. 1606 S. C.

Delivered by Bro. Thian Seong Yee, Senior Deacon
Opening

Right Worshipful Master, Brethren, and to Arun's family who are with us this morning -- I have been asked to say a few words about our Brother. I will do my best. But I want to be honest with you: I am not sure five minutes is enough to hold thirty-three years of friendship. So I will give you what I can.

The meeting

Ampang, 1993. A young drug salesman walks into a new OBGYN clinic. He has a briefcase full of painkiller samples and not much else to say. That salesman was me. The doctor was Arun Bag -- freshly set up, building a practice from nothing. I had exhausted my sales pitch, so out of sheer boredom, I mentioned this new technology I had been reading about. The internet. I expected him to nod politely and reach for his next patient file. Instead, he leaned forward and said what I would hear him say a hundred times over the next thirty-three years: "Tell me more." For the next hour, we forgot the clinic, the samples, the schedule. We were two men imagining a future that did not exist yet.

That was Arun. "Tell me more." Three words that summed up the man. He was always curious, always leaning in. He turned strangers into friends. He found the thing that connected you and held onto it. And that conversation -- about what comes next, about what is possible -- continued for thirty-three years, over yong tau fu in Ampang, over steam fish head with Gordon, Neo, and Voon Loong. At the time, I had no idea they were all Brethren -- to me they were just Arun's friends who gathered for a good meal. That is the kind of company he kept, and the kind of world he quietly drew you into. He delivered my second son. That is how close we were -- he was not just my friend, he was the man I trusted with my family.

The doctor and family man

Arun trained in London when he had almost no money. He worked night shifts in hospitals to pay his way -- and here is the part he was always proud to tell: he went to London with next to nothing, and by the time he came home, he had a surplus. Not because anyone helped him. Because he was that disciplined, that resourceful, that determined. He started in orthopaedics, moved to OBGYN, built a respected practice. None of it was handed to him. He shaped himself with the same discipline we are taught to apply chisel to stone -- not polished in the way of vanity, but polished in the way of purpose. A man who had made himself useful.

Dr. Kumari, his wife, a paediatrician, walked that road with him. Together they raised three daughters who are now doctors and professionals in their own right, in Australia and the UK. He was so proud of them -- if you ever saw Arun watching his daughters perform their dance recitals, you saw a man completely lost in joy. And even as cancer took more and more from his body over six years, he kept flying to see them. He kept holding his grandchildren. He was about to migrate to be closer to them when the diagnosis came. Life changed the plan. He did not let it change his love.

The Mason

That same day in 1993, I noticed something unusual in his consultation room. Objects I did not recognise -- symbols, shapes, something that looked like it belonged to another century. I asked what they were. He said, "Freemasonry," and smiled. Nothing more. He did not explain. He did not invite. He waited. Brethren, he waited thirty years. When he finally proposed me for the Craft, with Gordon as seconder, it was because he had decided -- in his own time, by his own judgement -- that I was ready. That single act of patience changed my life. This lodge, these friendships, this journey -- I owe it to him.

His year in the East came during COVID-19, the same season he was diagnosed with non-small cell lung cancer. He led this lodge from a screen, through darkness, and the Brothers knew what he was carrying. He did not ask for sympathy. He showed up. And even through COVID lockdowns, even through his own cancer treatment, he was still thinking about others -- helping his schoolmate Tony run the soup kitchen, making sure the people who depended on those meals were not forgotten.

But what I will remember most about Arun as a Mason is this: when a Brother was struggling -- with health, with finances, with life -- Arun did not wait to be asked. He would quietly pull that Brother aside after a meeting, or take him for a meal, and simply ask how he was. Privately. Without an audience. That was his charity. Not just the soup kitchen with Tony, not just the lodge contributions, not just the free medical advice he gave to any Brother who needed it -- though he gave all of that, generously and without counting. It was the private word. The quiet hand on the shoulder. The meal where he listened more than he spoke.

The last visit

Two months ago, a dozen of us went to see him. I did not know it would be the last time. He was tired, yes. But he was still Arun. Still sharp. Still planning. He told us he wanted to try the next round of chemo. Six years of fighting and he was still looking ahead. I think about that visit every day now. I am grateful I was there. I am grateful we all were.

To the family

Dr. Kumari -- your husband was one of the finest men I have ever known. To Arun's daughters -- the lodge that your father helped to build is stronger for his presence and poorer for his absence. His goodness lives in you. We will not forget him. I will not forget him.

Close

Brethren, the sun has set on our Brother's earthly labour. His column is lowered. But the temple stands. And in every act of charity, every quiet word to a Brother in need, every moment of patience, the work of Brother Dr. Arun Bag continues. From labour to refreshment. From darkness to light. So mote it be.
